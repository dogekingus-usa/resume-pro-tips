How to Write a LinkedIn Summary That Gets You Hired in 2026: 5 Templates

Published: May 21, 2026 | Reading time: 8 min

Your LinkedIn summary is the most-read section of your entire profile. According to LinkedIn's internal data, profiles with a complete summary section receive up to 40% more messages from recruiters and appear in 3x more search results. Yet most professionals either leave theirs blank, write a boring list of job duties, or fall into the trap of vague self-promotion that says nothing and impresses no one.

In 2026, with over 875 million LinkedIn users and recruiters spending an average of just 7 seconds scanning a profile, your summary needs to do more than describe you -- it needs to sell you, instantly and memorably. The difference between a profile that generates interview requests and one that collects digital dust comes down to three things: clarity, credibility, and a compelling story.

In this guide, you'll learn the exact framework for writing a LinkedIn summary that gets you hired, plus 5 proven templates you can adapt for your specific situation -- whether you're a career changer, an executive, a new graduate, or a seasoned professional looking for your next opportunity.

The Anatomy of a High-Converting LinkedIn Summary

Before we get to the templates, let's break down the structure every effective LinkedIn summary follows. Think of it as a formula you can plug into, regardless of your industry or experience level:

ElementPurposeLength
HookGrab attention in the first 2 lines above the "see more" fold1-2 sentences
Value PropositionState what you do, who you help, and the results you deliver2-3 sentences
Proof PointsBack up your claims with specific metrics, achievements, or credentials2-4 bullet points or 2 sentences
DifferentiatorWhat makes your approach unique -- your methodology, perspective, or combination of skills1-2 sentences
Call to ActionTell the reader what to do next -- connect, message, visit your website, download your portfolio1 sentence
LinkedIn's Hidden Rule: Only the first 2-3 lines of your summary (about 200-300 characters) are visible without clicking "see more." That's roughly 30-45 words. If those lines don't hook the reader, the rest of your summary -- no matter how brilliant -- will never be read. Write your hook first. Spend 80% of your editing time on it.

Template 1: The Career Changer

Use this template if you're transitioning to a new industry or role. The goal is to emphasize your transferable skills and unique perspective while acknowledging -- but not apologizing for -- your unconventional background.

?? Template: Career Changer

Hook:
"I help [target industry/function] teams solve [specific problem] -- a skill I developed over [X] years in [previous industry]."

Body:
"After [X] years driving results in [previous industry] -- where I [top achievement with metric] -- I'm now applying my expertise to [target industry]. I've found that [unique insight from previous industry] translates powerfully into [target industry context], enabling me to [specific value you bring].

Recently completed [bridge credential] and built [portfolio project or side project] that demonstrates my ability to [target industry skill]."

Call to Action:
"I'm actively exploring opportunities in [target role] where I can bring a fresh perspective and [key strength]. Let's connect if you're building [type of team/company]."

Example:
"I help product teams make data-informed decisions -- a skill I developed over 6 years building analytics infrastructure in fintech. After driving a 23% reduction in customer churn through predictive modeling at PayFlow, I'm now applying my data strategy expertise to the healthtech space. Recently completed the Health Informatics certification from Stanford Online and built a patient outcomes dashboard that earned recognition from the Digital Health Association. I'm exploring product analytics roles at mission-driven healthtech companies. Let's connect if you're building the future of digital health."

Template 2: The Executive / Senior Leader

Senior leaders need a summary that communicates authority, strategic vision, and impact -- not tactical day-to-day activities. Focus on the scale and scope of your leadership, the transformations you've led, and the teams you've built.

?? Template: Executive / Senior Leader

Hook:
"[Industry/Function] executive with [X years] of experience driving [specific outcome] at [scale -- revenue, team size, company stage]."

Body:
"I specialize in [area of expertise] -- helping [type of organization] achieve [specific result]. Throughout my career, I've [top achievement: revenue growth, team scaling, product launch, acquisition, etc.].

My leadership philosophy centers on [your leadership principle]. I believe that [your unique management or strategic philosophy]. Under my leadership, [team or org achievement with metrics]."

Call to Action:
"I'm currently [situation: advising, board member, seeking next role]. If you're building or scaling [type of organization], I'd welcome the chance to connect."

Example:
"Product and technology executive with 18+ years driving revenue growth and digital transformation at companies ranging from Series B startups to Fortune 100 enterprises. I specialize in turning fragmented product portfolios into cohesive platforms that deliver measurable business outcomes -- most recently leading a $50M product line that grew 34% YoY. My leadership philosophy: hire people smarter than you, give them extreme clarity on the mission, and get out of their way. I'm currently advising two early-stage SaaS companies and seeking a CPO or VP Product role where I can build high-performing teams and transformative products."

Template 3: The Entry-Level / New Graduate

If you have limited work experience, focus on your education, relevant projects, internships, and the value you'll bring as a motivated learner. Energy and potential matter more than tenure.

?? Template: Entry-Level / New Graduate

Hook:
"Recent [degree] graduate passionate about [field/industry] -- with hands-on experience in [key skill area] through [internship/project/extracurricular]."

Body:
"During my [degree program] at [university], I discovered my passion for [specific area]. I've since gained practical experience through [internship/project/volunteer role], where I [achievement with metric or impact statement].

I bring [X key strengths: e.g., analytical thinking, creative problem-solving, technical skills] that I'm eager to apply in a [type of role or company environment]. I'm a fast learner who thrives in [type of environment]. My [specific project or accomplishment] earned [recognition/grade/award] and taught me [lesson or skill]."

Call to Action:
"I'm actively seeking [type of role] opportunities where I can contribute, learn, and grow. Let's connect if you're looking for someone who brings [top two qualities] and a genuine passion for [field]."

Example:
"Recent Computer Science graduate passionate about building accessible software -- with hands-on experience in full-stack development through a product design internship at a Series A startup. During my CS program at UCLA, I built an accessibility-focused browser extension that reached 2,000+ users and won 'Best Social Impact Project' at the annual hackathon. I bring strong problem-solving abilities, a user-centered design mindset, and proficiency in React, Python, and SQL. I'm seeking junior software engineering roles where I can contribute to products that make a real difference. Let's connect if your team values curiosity, inclusive design, and clean code."

Template 4: The Mid-Career Professional (Seeking Growth)

This is the most common situation -- you have solid experience but aren't a C-level executive. Your summary needs to show progression, expertise, and ambition without overselling.

?? Template: Mid-Career Professional

Hook:
"[Role/function] professional with [X years] of experience helping [type of clients/companies] achieve [specific outcome]."

Body:
"My career has been defined by [one consistent theme -- e.g., building systems, driving growth, solving complex problems]. At [current/most recent company], I [top achievement with metric]. Previously, I [previous achievement] at [previous company].

What sets me apart is [your unique differentiator -- e.g., the combination of creative and analytical skills, experience across multiple industries, a specific methodology]. I'm at my best when [your ideal work scenario]."

Call to Action:
"I'm open to [type of opportunities] -- particularly in [specific industry or company type]. If you're looking for a [role type] who can [key value], let's connect and explore how I can contribute."

Example:
"Marketing professional with 8 years of experience helping B2B SaaS companies generate qualified leads and shorten sales cycles. At CloudScale, I built the demand generation engine from scratch, driving 185% pipeline growth in 18 months while reducing cost-per-lead by 34%. Previously, I led content marketing at a $20M Series A startup through its Series B raise. What sets me apart is my ability to bridge creative content strategy with rigorous data analysis -- I don't just write campaigns, I build attribution models that prove their ROI. I'm open to senior marketing manager or director roles at growth-stage B2B SaaS companies. Let's connect if you need a marketer who treats pipeline as a measurable system, not a creative exercise."

Template 5: The Return-to-Work / Career Break Professional

If you're returning after a career break -- parental leave, caregiving, health, travel, or layoff -- this template frames your gap as a period of growth, not absence. It emphasizes readiness and continuous development.

?? Template: Return-to-Work / Career Break

Hook:
"[Former role/function] professional returning to the workforce after a [planned/intentional] career break -- bringing [X years] of experience and renewed focus."

Body:
"After [X years in previous field], I took a [length] career break to [reason -- be honest but brief]. During that time, I stayed engaged with my field through [activities: certifications, freelance projects, volunteering, coursework].

I returned to [field/industry] with [specific new skills or credentials earned during the break] and a clearer sense of what I want in my next role. My background in [key expertise] combined with [new skill] makes me uniquely positioned to [value proposition for target companies]."

Call to Action:
"I'm actively pursuing [type of role] opportunities and would love to connect with [type of professionals: recruiters, hiring managers, peers in the field]. Let's talk if you're looking for someone with [top two strengths] combined with the perspective that comes from [diverse life or professional experience]."

Example:
"Project management professional returning to the workforce after an intentional 18-month career break for family caregiving -- bringing 7 years of experience and renewed energy. During my break, I earned my PMP certification, completed a Google Data Analytics certificate, and volunteered as a project coordinator for a local education nonprofit. I returned to project management with stronger analytical capabilities and a clearer focus on mission-driven work. My background in managing $2M+ cross-functional projects combined with new data analytics skills positions me to deliver insights alongside execution. I'm actively pursuing senior project manager or program manager roles in the education or nonprofit technology space. Let's connect if you value project leaders who combine operational rigor with genuine mission alignment."

5 Quick Wins to Optimize Your LinkedIn Summary Right Now

Beyond the templates, these micro-optimizations will make your summary more effective starting today:

  1. Write in first person: "I help teams..." not "John helps teams..." First person feels more authentic and conversational. Save third-person for your headline only.
  2. Use emojis sparingly (2-3 max): A single emoji in your hook can boost engagement, but too many look unprofessional. Use them as visual signposts, not decoration.
  3. Include keywords from your target job descriptions: Recruiters search LinkedIn using the same keywords they use in job descriptions. Incorporate them naturally into your summary to appear in more searches.
  4. Add media to your summary section: LinkedIn allows you to attach PDFs, links, images, and videos to your summary. Add your portfolio, a recent presentation, or a PDF of your resume. Profiles with media receive 7x more messages.
  5. Update your summary every 90 days: Even if nothing major changes, refresh your summary quarterly. LinkedIn's algorithm favors recently updated profiles in search results.
The 5-Second Test: Show your LinkedIn summary to someone who doesn't know you. Ask them to read it for 5 seconds -- then close the tab and tell you what you do. If they can't, your summary isn't clear enough. Rewrite until it passes this test.

Final Thoughts

Your LinkedIn summary is not a biography. It's a marketing document with one job: to convince a busy recruiter or hiring manager -- in under 10 seconds -- that they should look at the rest of your profile, send you a message, or invite you to apply.

The 5 templates above are proven frameworks that work across industries, experience levels, and career situations. But they're only as good as the specific details you fill them with. Generic summaries get ignored. Specific, quantified, authentic summaries get replies.

Choose the template that fits your situation. Customize it with your real numbers, achievements, and personality. Run it through the 5-second test. Then update it and watch your LinkedIn engagement transform.

Your next career opportunity is one well-written summary away.
